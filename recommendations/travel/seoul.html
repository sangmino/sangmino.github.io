Seoul, South Korea

Introduction

As someone from Daegu, I should mention that I'm also a visitor to Seoul in many ways. While this guide reflects my experiences and research, Seoul is a city that even many Koreans find constantly evolving and surprising. Like many Koreans from outside the capital, I have a complex relationship with Seoul: it's familiar yet foreign, overwhelming yet exciting. I hope this guide helps you navigate this fascinating city with both its mainstream attractions and hidden gems.

Seoul is a sprawling metropolis of 10 million people (25 million if you count the greater metropolitan area), built around the mighty Han River. This river creates a natural division between Gangnam (south) and Gangbuk (north), each with its distinct character. Gangnam is the modern face of Seoul, with its gleaming office towers and upscale neighborhoods, while Gangbuk holds most of the city's historic sites and government institutions.

The city blends ultra-modern infrastructure with historic temples and palaces. Its extensive subway system connects all corners of the city, making it surprisingly navigable despite its size. Most academic institutions are concentrated in specific districts: Seoul National University in Gwanak, Korea University in Anam, and Yonsei University in Sinchon.

A Few Tips

Seoul differs from Western cities in several practical aspects.

  • The city operates on a near 24-hour schedule, particularly in commercial and entertainment districts.
  • Public transportation is extensive and highly reliable, though service typically ends around midnight.
  • While local food and transport costs are moderate by international standards, luxury goods and high-end dining are priced similarly to other major global cities.
  • The service industry operates without tipping, and credit cards are widely accepted except in traditional markets and some small establishments.
  • Cultural norms and business etiquette differ significantly from Western practices, particularly regarding age hierarchy and group dynamics.

Navigation & Transport

  • Download Naver Maps or Kakao Maps. Google Maps is unreliable in Korea.
  • Get a T-money card from any convenience store or subway station (₩4,000 deposit, refundable) for subway and bus transport.
  • Subway is extensive and runs from ~5:30AM to midnight.
  • Taxis are affordable and use reliable meters. Download the Kakao T app for easy hailing. Uber also works, but you will not be able to pay with Apple Pay.

Money & Payments

  • Korea is very card-friendly. Even small restaurants usually accept cards.
  • Traditional markets and some older establishments may be cash-only.
  • No tipping culture. Tipping can even be considered awkward or offensive.

Price Levels (as of 2025)

  • Casual meal: ₩10,000-15,000 ($7-11)
  • Korean BBQ (pork): ₩15,000-25,000 ($11-18) per person
  • Korean BBQ (beef/hanwoo): ₩35,000-60,000 ($25-45) per person
  • High-end dining: ₩60,000-120,000+ ($45-90+) per person

Restaurant Etiquette

  • Most restaurants serve water and banchan (side dishes) for free.
  • Refills of banchan and water are typically free. Just ask.
  • Some popular places don't take reservations and may have long waits.
  • At Korean BBQ, staff will usually help with the grilling.
  • Don't pour your own drinks. Let others pour for you, and pour for others.
  • When dining with Korean colleagues, let the senior person lead.

Language

  • Basic English is understood in major areas but limited elsewhere.
  • Having key phrases written in Korean (especially food allergies/preferences) is helpful.
  • Most restaurants have pictures or English menus in tourist areas.

To Explore

The north contains most historical sites, government buildings, and older neighborhoods, while the south houses the modern business districts and entertainment zones. The city's extensive public transportation system, particularly its subway, connects these diverse areas efficiently. Major districts each serve distinct functions: Jongno for history and politics, Gangnam for business, Hongdae for youth culture, and Yeouido for finance.

Historic Seoul

  • Gyeongbokgung Palace (경복궁): Korea's grandest palace, built in 1395 as the main royal palace of the Joseon dynasty. Known for its changing of the guard ceremony and stunning mountain backdrop. Best visited in early morning to avoid crowds. 종로 Jongno
  • Changdeokgung Palace (창덕궁): UNESCO site featuring a "Secret Garden" (비원). More intimate than Gyeongbokgung, this palace was the preferred residence of many Joseon kings. Book Secret Garden tours in advance. 종로 Jongno
  • Bukchon Hanok Village (북촌한옥마을): A historic neighborhood filled with traditional Korean houses between the two palaces. Best viewed from the elevated spots near Gahoe-dong. Many houses are still private residences. 종로 Jongno
  • Jogyesa Temple (조계사): Center of Korean Buddhism, famous for its ancient trees and lantern festival on Buddha's birthday. Offers temple stay programs and regular meditation sessions. 종로 Jongno
  • Deoksugung Palace (덕수궁): Known for its unique stone-wall road and mix of traditional Korean and Western architecture. Beautiful when lit up at night. 중구 Jung-gu
  • Unhyeongung Palace (운현궁): Lesser-known palace where Emperor Gojong spent his childhood. More peaceful than main palaces. 종로 Jongno
  • Namsangol Hanok Village (남산골한옥마을): Five restored traditional Korean houses at the foot of Namsan Mountain. Offers traditional craft workshops. 중구 Jung-gu
  • Seodaemun Prison History Hall (서대문형무소역사관): Former prison turned museum, important site for understanding Korea's independence movement. 서대문 Seodaemun
  • Bongeunsa Temple (봉은사): Ancient Buddhist temple in modern Gangnam. Striking contrast with COEX building across the street. 강남 Gangnam

Modern Seoul

  • Dongdaemun Design Plaza (동대문디자인플라자): Zaha Hadid's iconic building, hosts fashion shows and design exhibitions. Spectacular at night with LED roses. 동대문 Dongdaemun
  • Lotte World Tower (롯데월드타워): Korea's tallest building (123 floors). Glass-floor sky deck offers breathtaking views. Book sunset time slots in advance. 잠실 Jamsil
  • Starfield COEX Mall (스타필드 코엑스몰): Massive underground complex with the Instagram-famous Starfield Library. Connected to multiple subway lines and hotels. 삼성 Samseong
  • Yeouido (여의도): Korea's Wall Street, home to major financial institutions. Best during cherry blossom season or evening river views. 여의도 Yeouido
  • Common Ground (커먼그라운드): World's largest container shopping mall, popular with young crowds. 건대 Konkuk
  • Gangnam Station Area (강남역): Heart of modern Seoul, endless shopping and dining options. 강남 Gangnam
  • Seoul Sky (서울스카이): Observation deck in Lotte World Tower, highest point in Korea. 잠실 Jamsil
  • Times Square (타임스퀘어): Massive shopping complex in Yeongdeungpo, modern architecture. 영등포 Yeongdeungpo
  • Sevit Island (세빛섬): Three floating islands on Han River, beautiful night views. 반포 Banpo
  • Lotte World (롯데월드): Major indoor/outdoor theme park with rides, ice skating, and Folk Museum. Connected to Lotte World Tower and aquarium. 잠실 Jamsil
  • Olympic Park (올림픽공원): Built for 1988 Olympics, now a popular venue for K-pop concerts. Features sculpture gardens, sports facilities, and SOMA art museum. 송파 Songpa

Cultural Experiences

  • National Museum of Korea (국립중앙박물관): Houses over 330,000 pieces. Free admission, closed Mondays. Plan at least half a day. 용산 Yongsan
  • Leeum Museum (리움미술관): Samsung's art museum complex, mixing traditional Korean art with contemporary works. Architecture alone worth the visit. 한남 Hannam
  • Gwangjang Market (광장시장): Korea's oldest remaining market, famous for bindaetteok (mung bean pancakes) and textile shops. Best experienced during lunch hours. 종로 Jongno
  • Insadong (인사동): Traditional culture street, best for souvenirs and tea houses. Visit on Sunday when the main road becomes pedestrian-only. 종로 Jongno
  • National Folk Museum (국립민속박물관): Located inside Gyeongbokgung, excellent for understanding daily life in historical Korea. 종로 Jongno
  • War Memorial of Korea (전쟁기념관): Comprehensive museum about Korean War. Free admission, impressive outdoor exhibition. 용산 Yongsan
  • Seoul Museum of History (서울역사박물관): Chronicles Seoul's transformation from ancient times. Good introduction to city's development. 종로 Jongno
  • Dongdaemun Market (동대문시장): 24-hour shopping district, especially for textiles and fashion. 동대문 Dongdaemun
  • Namdaemun Market (남대문시장): One of Korea's oldest and largest traditional markets, selling everything from food to clothing to kitchenware. Famous for galchi-jorim (braised cutlassfish) alley. 중구 Jung-gu
  • Bukchon Cultural Center (북촌문화센터): Offers hands-on traditional Korean cultural experiences. 종로 Jongno

K-Culture & Entertainment

  • SM COEX Artium (에스엠코엑스아티움): K-pop entertainment company's exhibition space and merchandise store. Live hologram concerts and interactive exhibits featuring popular SM artists. 삼성 Samseong
  • Hybe Insight (하이브 인사이트): BTS's entertainment company's museum. Interactive music experiences and behind-the-scenes exhibits of K-pop production. 용산 Yongsan
  • K-Star Road (케이스타로드): Street in Gangnam featuring K-pop agencies and giant bear statues representing different K-pop groups. 강남 Gangnam
  • MBC World (MBC월드): Broadcasting station theme park with virtual reality experiences and K-drama sets. 상암 Sangam
  • Hongdae (홍대): Seoul's youth culture hub near Hongik University, known for street performances, K-pop dance busking, indie music venues, and art galleries. The area around "Hongdae Playground" comes alive at night with buskers and dancers. 홍대/신촌 Hongdae
  • Sinchon (신촌): University district home to Yonsei, Ewha, and Sogang universities. More student-focused than neighboring Hongdae, with affordable restaurants, PC bangs, and late-night study cafes. The main street connects to Ewha Womans University's beautiful campus. 홍대/신촌 Hongdae
  • Yeonnam-dong (연남동): Quieter residential area adjacent to Hongdae with trendy cafes, brunch spots, and the popular Gyeongui Line Forest Park, a converted railway turned urban green space. 홍대/신촌 Hongdae

Jjimjilbang (찜질방) Culture

Korean bathhouses that combine saunas, hot baths, and communal sleeping areas. Open 24 hours, they're a unique cultural experience and a budget-friendly place to spend the night. Guests wear provided pajamas and can enjoy various heated rooms, snacks, and entertainment.

  • Dragon Hill Spa (드래곤힐스파): Most foreigner-friendly, with multiple themed rooms and outdoor pools. 용산 Yongsan
  • Siloam Sauna (실로암사우나): Local favorite near Seoul Station, more authentic experience. 서울역 Seoul Station
  • Spaland COEX (스파랜드): Upscale option with premium facilities and quieter atmosphere. 삼성 Samseong

Hanbok Rental (한복 대여)

Renting traditional Korean clothing (hanbok) is popular for palace visits. Many rental shops near Gyeongbokgung and Bukchon offer 2-4 hour rentals (₩15,000-30,000) with hair styling. Wearing hanbok grants free admission to major palaces. Book ahead during peak seasons.

Korean Entertainment Culture

Experience how Koreans socialize and unwind:

  • Noraebang (노래방): Private karaoke rooms where groups rent rooms by the hour (₩15,000-30,000/hr). Available 24 hours, often with tambourines and disco lights. Coin noraebang (동전노래방) offers budget solo singing.
  • PC Bang (PC방): Gaming cafes with high-end computers, comfortable chairs, and food service. Around ₩1,500-2,000/hour. Experience Korea's esports culture firsthand. Found everywhere, open 24 hours.

Nature and City Views

  • Namsan Seoul Tower (남산서울타워): Iconic tower with citywide views. Popular for its "love locks" and cable car ride. 남산 Namsan
  • Seoul Forest (서울숲): Seoul's answer to Central Park. Beautiful in spring and fall, has deer enclosure. 성수 Seongsu
  • Han River Parks (한강공원):
    • Yeouido Hangang Park (여의도한강공원): Most popular, great for picnics. 여의도 Yeouido
    • Banpo Hangang Park (반포한강공원): Famous for Rainbow Fountain Bridge. 반포 Banpo
    • Ttukseom Hangang Park (뚝섬한강공원): Water sports and youth culture. 성수 Seongsu
  • Bukhansan National Park (북한산국립공원): Mountain hiking within city limits, spectacular views of Seoul. 북한산 Bukhansan
  • Cheonggyecheon Stream (청계천): Restored 11km urban stream running through downtown. Popular walking path with art installations, especially beautiful at night. Starts near Gwanghwamun. 종로 Jongno

Trendy Neighborhoods

  • Seongsu-dong (성수동): "Brooklyn of Seoul." Industrial buildings converted to cafes, art galleries, and independent fashion brands. 성수 Seongsu
  • Euljiro (을지로): Retro vibes. Hidden bars in old printing shops, industrial aesthetic cafes, and traditional mom-and-pop shops. 을지로 Euljiro
  • Garosu-gil (가로수길): Trendy shopping street with designer boutiques, Instagram-worthy cafes, and beauty shops. 신사 Sinsa
  • Itaewon (이태원): Seoul's most international district. Diverse food scene, craft beer bars, vintage shops, and LGBTQ+ friendly Homo Hill. Adjacent Gyeongnidan-gil has trendy cafes and restaurants. 이태원 Itaewon
  • Myeongdong (명동): Korea's biggest shopping and beauty district. Wall-to-wall cosmetics stores, street food stalls, and department stores. Crowded but essential for K-beauty shopping. 명동 Myeongdong

To Eat

In general, Korean meals are structured around a main dish accompanied by various side dishes (반찬, banchan). These side dishes are refillable and create a complete dining experience. Most restaurants specialize in specific dishes rather than offering extensive menus, a sign of expertise and dedication to craft.

Recommendations for Vegetarians

While Korea's contemporary food culture is notably meat-centric, the country has a rich tradition of vegetarian cuisine rooted in Buddhist temple food (사찰음식, sachal eumsik). Temple cuisine, developed over centuries by Buddhist monks, is completely plant-based and eschews strong aromatics like garlic and onions. It showcases sophisticated techniques for bringing out natural flavors through fermentation, dehydration, and careful seasoning.

While finding strictly vegetarian food in regular restaurants can be challenging (even seemingly vegetable dishes often contain fish sauce or meat stock), Korea offers unique vegetarian experiences through temple restaurants and a growing modern plant-based movement. The cuisine emphasizes seasonal mountain vegetables (산나물, sannammul), various types of mushrooms, and numerous preparations of soybeans including tofu (두부), offering complex flavors without relying on meat.

A helpful resource: veganseoul.com

Pork

Korean pork culture centers around samgyeopsal (삼겹살, grilled pork belly), a national favorite that's as much about the communal dining experience as the meat itself. The most prized variety is Jeju Black Pork, raised on Jeju Island, known for its exceptional marbling and clean taste.

Beyond grilled preparations, Koreans excel at braised and boiled pork dishes. Jokbal (족발, braised pig's feet) features tender, gelatinous meat in a soy-based sauce, popular as drinking food. Bossam (보쌈, boiled pork wraps) pairs sliced pork belly with kimchi and raw garlic in lettuce or perilla leaves. Dwaeji-gukbap (돼지국밥, pork soup with rice) is a hearty, comforting dish originating from Busan. And don't miss jeyuk-bokkeum (제육볶음), spicy stir-fried pork that's a lunch staple.

Beef

Korean beef, or hanwoo (한우), is celebrated for its exceptional marbling and deep flavor profile, rivaling Japan's wagyu. The grading system (1++ being the highest) reflects the intense focus on quality. Korean beef culture emphasizes minimal seasoning, often just salt and sesame oil, to highlight the meat's natural flavors.

Beyond barbecue, beef appears in dishes like yukhoe (육회, raw beef tartare) and gomtang (곰탕, rich beef bone soup), each showcasing different aspects of the meat.

Chicken

Korean chicken preparation balances two distinct traditions: the ancient (medical food like samgyetang, 삼계탕, ginseng chicken soup) and the modern (Korean fried chicken culture).

Korean fried chicken, or chimaek (치맥, chicken and beer), represents a post-war innovation that's become a global phenomenon, known for its ultra-crispy double-fried technique and variety of glazes. Dakgalbi (닭갈비), spicy stir-fried chicken with vegetables and rice cakes, is a Chuncheon specialty now popular throughout Seoul. The country's chicken dishes often incorporate ginseng, garlic, and medicinal herbs, reflecting traditional Korean medical theories.

Seafood

As a peninsula, Korea's seafood cuisine reflects its three-sided coastal geography. The country is particularly famous for its hwe (회, raw fish), seafood stews like haemultang (해물탕), and fermented preparations like jeotgal (젓갈).

Live octopus (산낙지), though not for the faint-hearted, remains a unique delicacy. Korean seafood culture emphasizes freshness to an extreme degree: many restaurants have live tanks, and markets like Noryangjin operate 24/7 to maintain the freshest possible supply.

Specific Dish Recommendations

Rice Dishes (밥류)

  • Bibimbap (비빔밥): Mixed rice bowl topped with vegetables, egg, meat, and gochujang. Each ingredient arranged separately before mixing. The dolsot (stone pot) version creates crispy rice.
  • Kimchi Bokkeumbap (김치볶음밥): Stir-fried rice with aged kimchi, typically includes pork or spam. Perfect balance of spicy and savory.

Soups & Stews (국/찌개)

  • Kimchi Jjigae (김치찌개): Spicy stew made with aged kimchi and pork. Deep, complex flavors from fermented kimchi.
  • Seolleongtang (설렁탕): Milky beef bone soup simmered for hours. Served with rice, green onions, and salt on the side to season to taste.
  • Sundubu Jjigae (순두부찌개): Soft tofu stew with seafood or meat in spicy broth. Often served bubbling hot with raw egg.
  • Samgyetang (삼계탕): Whole young chicken stuffed with ginseng, rice, and medicinal herbs. Traditional summer health food.

Noodles (면류)

  • Naengmyeon (냉면): Cold buckwheat noodles in beef broth (mul-naengmyeon) or spicy sauce (bibim-naengmyeon). Topped with sliced pear, cucumber, and egg.
  • Japchae (잡채): Sweet potato noodles stir-fried with vegetables and meat. Common holiday dish.
  • Kalguksu (칼국수): Hand-cut wheat noodles in rich chicken or seafood broth. Often served with dumplings.
  • Jjajangmyeon (짜장면): Korean-Chinese black bean noodles. Sweet and savory sauce made from chunjang (black bean paste). A delivery and jajangmyeon-jip staple.

BBQ & Grilled Dishes (구이)

  • Samgyeopsal (삼겹살): Grilled pork belly, usually unseasoned. Eaten with lettuce wraps, garlic, and sauces.
  • Galbi (갈비): Marinated beef short ribs. Sweet and savory marinade with soy sauce, garlic, and fruit.
  • Bulgogi (불고기): Thinly sliced marinated beef, traditionally grilled but also served as soup or hot pot.

Street Food (길거리 음식)

  • Tteokbokki (떡볶이): Spicy rice cakes in gochujang sauce. Sometimes includes fish cakes and boiled eggs.
  • Hotteok (호떡): Sweet pancakes filled with brown sugar, nuts, and cinnamon.
  • Bungeoppang (붕어빵): Fish-shaped pastry filled with sweet red bean paste.
  • Gimbap (김밥): Korean-style sushi rolls with vegetables and meat.
  • Sundae (순대): Korean blood sausage stuffed with glass noodles and barley. Often served with tteokbokki or in soup (sundae-guk).
  • Mandu (만두): Korean dumplings, steamed (jjin-mandu), fried (gun-mandu), or in soup (mandu-guk). Fillings vary from pork to kimchi.
  • Gyeran-ppang (계란빵): Egg bread, a warm street snack with a whole egg baked into sweet bread.

Drinking Food (안주)

  • Korean Fried Chicken (치킨): Double-fried for extra crispiness, various sauces available.
  • Pajeon (파전): Savory pancake with green onions, seafood, or kimchi. Haemul-pajeon (seafood pancake) is especially popular.
  • Budae Jjigae (부대찌개): "Army stew" featuring American military base ingredients like spam and hot dogs, mixed with Korean ingredients.
  • Jokbal (족발): Braised pig's feet, sliced thin and served with salted shrimp sauce. Classic late-night drinking food.
  • Tangsuyuk (탕수육): Korean-Chinese sweet and sour pork. Crispy battered pork with tangy sauce, served with jjajangmyeon.

Drinks

Korean drinking culture is deeply social, with its own etiquette and rituals. Pouring drinks for others (never for yourself) and receiving with two hands when someone senior pours for you are fundamental customs. The phrase "one shot" (원샷) often accompanies toasts.

Alcoholic Beverages (술)

  • Soju (소주): Korea's national liquor, a clear distilled spirit traditionally made from rice. Modern versions hover around 16-20% ABV. Jinro and Chamisul are ubiquitous brands, though craft soju is gaining popularity.
  • Makgeolli (막걸리): Milky, slightly sweet rice wine with a mild fizz. Often served in brass bowls. Pairs exceptionally well with pajeon on rainy days, a beloved Korean tradition.
  • Maekju (맥주): Korean beer. Cass and Hite dominate the market, though the craft beer scene has exploded in recent years, particularly in Itaewon and Gyeongnidan.
  • Somaek (소맥): A popular combination of soju and beer mixed together. The ratio is debated but typically around 3:7 soju to beer.
  • Cheongju (청주): Clear rice wine, more refined than makgeolli. Often compared to sake but with distinct Korean characteristics.

Non-Alcoholic Drinks (음료)

  • Sikhye (식혜): Sweet rice punch, a traditional dessert drink often served at the end of meals or at jjimjilbangs. Refreshing and mildly sweet with floating rice grains.
  • Sujeonggwa (수정과): Cinnamon and dried persimmon punch. Deep amber color with warming spices, typically served cold.
  • Yuja-cha (유자차): Citron tea made from preserved yuja fruit. Popular remedy for colds.
  • Bori-cha (보리차): Roasted barley tea, served cold in summer and hot in winter. Often the default "water" at restaurants.
  • Banana Milk (바나나맛 우유): Iconic Korean banana-flavored milk in a distinctive squat bottle. A convenience store staple since 1974.

Cafe Culture

Korea has one of the highest cafe densities in the world, and coffee culture has become deeply embedded in daily life. Beyond global chains, Seoul is home to countless independent cafes, each with unique concepts ranging from animal cafes to study cafes (스터디카페) where students pay by the hour. Traditional tea houses (찻집, chatjip) in areas like Insadong offer a quieter alternative, serving green tea, omija tea, and other traditional Korean teas.
